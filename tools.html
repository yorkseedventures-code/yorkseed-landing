Frequently Asked Questions

SAFE notes & convertible notes
explained simply

What is a SAFE note?
A SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) is a financing instrument that gives an investor the right to receive equity in a future priced round. Unlike a convertible note, it does not accrue interest and has no maturity date. It converts into shares when a qualifying financing event occurs, at either the valuation cap price or a discounted price — whichever is lower.
How does a SAFE note convert to equity?
A SAFE converts at the lower of the valuation cap price (investment amount divided by the cap) or the discounted price (round price minus the agreed discount rate). If neither applies, it converts at the priced round share price with no benefit to the investor. Use the calculator above to model your exact numbers.
What is a valuation cap on a SAFE?
A valuation cap sets the maximum valuation at which an investor's SAFE will convert to equity. If your priced round valuation exceeds the cap, the investor converts at the cap price — getting more shares than new investors paying the full round price. It rewards early investors for taking early risk.
What is the difference between a SAFE note and a convertible note?
The key differences: (1) Convertible notes accrue interest; SAFEs do not. (2) Convertible notes have a maturity date; SAFEs do not. (3) Convertible notes are technically debt instruments; SAFEs are not. Both can include a valuation cap and discount rate. Convertible notes typically result in the investor receiving more shares due to accrued interest.
What happens to a convertible note at maturity?
If no qualifying priced round has occurred by the maturity date, the outcome depends on your note terms. Common outcomes: (1) the investor demands repayment of principal plus accrued interest; (2) the note auto-converts at the cap or discount; or (3) both parties agree to extend. Always clarify this scenario in the term sheet before signing.
What is an MFN clause in a SAFE note?
MFN stands for Most Favoured Nation. An MFN clause means that if the company later issues a SAFE on better terms, the original investor automatically receives those better terms retroactively. It is generally founder-unfriendly — negotiate it out if you can.
