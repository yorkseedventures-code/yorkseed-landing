FAQ

SAFE notes & convertible notes
explained simply

What is a post-money SAFE and how is it different from pre-money?
A post-money SAFE (YC standard since 2018) locks the investor's ownership % at signing: Ownership = Investment ÷ Post-money cap. $1M on a $10M cap = exactly 10%. A pre-money SAFE calculates ownership at conversion based on share count, typically yielding slightly less (e.g. 9.09% on the same numbers). Post-money SAFEs now represent ~85% of all SAFEs signed globally per Carta 2024 data.
What happens if the next round valuation is below the SAFE cap?
This is the edge case most calculators miss. If the round valuation is below (or equal to) the cap, the cap no longer helps — the investor converts at the lower round price. This means they get more shares than the cap-implied ownership % — because their fixed investment converts at a cheaper price. For example: $1M on a $10M cap (10% locked). But if the round comes in at $6M pre-money, the investor converts at the round price and ends up owning more than 10%.
Why does stacking multiple SAFEs get complicated?
When multiple SAFEs convert simultaneously, each one's share issuance dilutes the others and the founders. A SAFE at a lower cap converts at a lower price (more shares), which reduces everyone else's percentage. The total dilution from stacked SAFEs is always higher than the simple sum of their cap ownership percentages. Use the Multi-SAFE Stack tab above to model this accurately.
What is the difference between a SAFE note and a convertible note?
(1) Convertible notes accrue interest; SAFEs do not. (2) Convertible notes have a maturity date; SAFEs do not. (3) Convertible notes are technically debt; SAFEs are not. Both can include a valuation cap and discount rate. Convertible notes result in more shares for investors due to accrued interest.
What is an MFN clause?
MFN (Most Favoured Nation) means if the company later issues a SAFE on better terms, the original investor automatically receives those better terms. It is founder-unfriendly. Per Carta 2024 data, MFN clauses appear primarily in institutional side letters (funds), not from angels.
What happens to a convertible note at maturity?
If no qualifying priced round occurs by maturity: (1) investor demands repayment of principal + interest; (2) note auto-converts at cap/discount; or (3) both parties agree to extend. Always clarify this in the term sheet. Build your fundraising timeline around the maturity date.
